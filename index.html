01
Introduction: Learning, Listening, Observation & Dialogue
The four pillars before we even begin designing

1.1 What is True Learning?

"I am talking about LEARNING — the insatiable curiosity that drives the adolescent boy to absorb everything he can see or hear or read about gasoline engines in order to improve the efficiency and speed of his cruiser." — Carl Rogers

Design Thinking begins not with a process, but with a state of mind. True learning is active, not passive. It is not the "lifeless, sterile" memorisation forced by conformity. It is driven by curiosity and the personal urgency to know.

The Learning Process Diagram (from Tatvasutra, Jain Scripture, 1st Century AD)

Three inputs — Observation, Experiential (direct experience), and Intention (purposeful direction) — feed into four processing layers:

  • Perception — Initial sensing of information
  • Conception — Forming mental models
  • Judgement — Evaluating and comparing
  • Retention — Storing into long-term knowledge

These four layers interact with a Knowledge Network containing: Memory, Logic, Recognition, and Inference nodes.

1.2 Listening: The Foundation of Real Learning

"If you listen with knowledge, with what you have learned, then actually you are not listening; you are interpreting; you are comparing, judging, evaluating..." — J. Krishnamurti

Krishnamurti's insight is radical: most of what we call "listening" is actually filtering — we match incoming information against our existing patterns and accept or reject accordingly. True listening requires suspending the past.

1.3 Dialogue vs. Discussion

This is one of the most actionable distinctions in the entire book:

Dimension Dialogue Discussion
Goal Design conversations; multiple alternatives Prove who is right
Mode Deep listening without bias or judgement "Boxing" of thoughts — me vs. others
Outcome Progressive; generative; inclusive Combative; stagnant; exclusive
Design Impact Converts frictional conflict into creative conflict Reinforces existing silos

1.4 Observation and Reading

  • Observation shifts your lens: Apple connected MP3 + phone + camera. IKEA removed non-value-added costs by having consumers assemble furniture — both are outcomes of observing patterns nobody else noticed.
  • Reading expands your horizon of thoughts. It creates new Knowledge Packets (KPs), perspectives, and helps connect dots across disciplines.
  • Reading also helps unearth blind spots — the "don't know what I don't know" zone.

2.1 Case Study: Sikhism as Design Thinking in Communities

A brilliant question was asked at a CII conference: "Can Design Thinking be applied to designing a community?" Arun Jain's answer: Sikhism is a masterclass in it.

7 Design Principles Embedded in Sikhism
  • Worship of Knowledge (not idols): Guru Granth Sahib is the authority — knowledge is divine. Best practices drawn from Hinduism and Islam are included (cross-industry thinking!).
  • Simplification of rituals: Major ceremonies consolidated; Sunday chosen for convenience (user experience design).
  • Brand Identity: Turban and beard — visually identifiable from a distance (brand differentiation).
  • Identity & Pride: "Jeet" (victory) built into names — Kamaljeet, Paramjeet (internal motivation design).
  • Balancing Pride with Humility: Kar Seva (cleaning shoes, floors), Langar (eating together irrespective of hierarchy) — counters ego's danger.
  • Community Bonding through Fun: Bhangra as a community dance — breaking inhibitions, building culture.

2.2 The Three Definitions of Design Thinking

Definition 1: The IDEO Model (Venn Diagram)

D Desirability
Human inspiration — What do people truly need?
F Feasibility
Technology ideation — What is technically possible?
V Viability
Business implementation — What can be sustainable?

Design Thinking lives at the intersection of all three. Missing any one creates an incomplete solution: desirable but infeasible, feasible but not wanted, or viable but not human-centered.

Definition 2: Roger Martin's Funnel (The Design of Business, 2009)

Mystery
A phenomenon that is not yet understood — "Why does this happen?" Raw, unexplained reality. The starting point of all innovation.
Heuristic
A working rule of thumb — a hypothesis that guides action without full certainty. "This seems to work, but we don't know exactly why."
Algorithm
A reliable, repeatable, codified process. "We know exactly what to do." Full mastery and institutionalisation.

Design Thinking is the process of moving knowledge down this funnel — from mystery toward algorithm — and also the courage to re-enter mystery when algorithms become outdated.

Definition 3: The Holistic Connect (Arun Jain's Own Model)

Understanding Requirements

Stated: What the customer says they want
Unstated: What they need but haven't articulated

Observing Patterns

Patterns: What consistently works
Anti-patterns: What consistently fails

Connecting the Dots

Within the Box: Improve within domain
Outside the Box: Cross-domain breakthroughs

Unearthing Blind Spots

"Don't know what I don't know" — The most dangerous gap is the unknown unknown.

2.3 Real-World Design Case Studies

Case Study: Devanagari Script (Hindi Alphabet)

The Hindi alphabet is a masterpiece of pattern-based design. Every letter group is organized by the physical source of the sound in the mouth:

  • क ख ग घ (ka, kha, ga, gha): Deep throat (कंठ)
  • च छ ज (cha, chha, ja): Tongue touching upper palate (तालु)
  • त थ द (ta, tha, da): Tongue touching upper surface
  • प फ ब भ (pa, pha, ba, bha): Lips
  • स श ष (sa, sha): Tongue touching teeth

Lesson: Observe the underlying pattern → Design the full system.

Case Study: South Indian Food Chain

Five design objectives: Nutrition · Variety · Simplicity · Easy digestion · Minimum waste. How the South Indian food chain achieves all five:

  • Adversity as Advantage: Tropical heat → fermentation as an ingredient (dosa batter)
  • Nutrition: 1/3 dal + 2/3 rice = balanced proteins + carbohydrates
  • Variety from one batter: Dosa / Uthappam / Idli — same input, different outputs (component-based thinking)
  • Simplicity: Cook rice once → 10+ dishes (tamarind rice, coconut rice, curd rice...)
  • Easy digestion: Pre-fermentation breaks down proteins and carbohydrates
  • Zero waste: Batter usable for multiple days; leftover rice becomes next morning's dish
Reflective Question

Think of a system you use daily (your morning routine, your workflow, your city's transport). Which of the three definitions of Design Thinking reveals the biggest design gap in it?

Unit 2 · Chapter 3
The Three Laws of Design Thinking

Universal principles that govern all great design. Violate them and no process can save you.

03
Three Laws of Design Thinking©
The governing physics of design — not guidelines, but laws
1st Law Less is More
Focus on what matters most. Remove redundancy. Simplification is the ultimate sophistication.
2nd Law Last 2% = 200%
The final differentiating detail (USP) creates a disproportionately massive impact.
3rd Law Theory of Prioritisation
Prioritisation must be exponential, not linear. Focus energy on 1000g items, not 10g distractions.

Law 1: Less is More

The book presents a memorable visual: a chaotic pile of different 3D shapes (cubes, spheres, pyramids) transforms into a clean, stacked column of identical cylinders. The message — complexity is not sophistication. True mastery is the ability to identify what is essential and eliminate everything else.

  • In products: Apple's iPod vs. other MP3 players of the era. Apple removed buttons, not added them.
  • In processes: An expert eye surgeon performs cataract surgery in 15 minutes (not 45) by eliminating redundant steps — that is expertise enabling simplification.
  • In communication: Define the problem in 5–7 words, not 5–7 paragraphs.

Law 2: Last 2% = 200% Impact

A bar chart in the book shows a small top layer labelled "2%" pointing to a towering column labelled "200%." The insight: the Unique Selling Proposition — the final, tiny differentiator — creates vastly disproportionate value. This is not about working 200% harder; it is about identifying the precise 2% that constitutes your differentiation.

Application Question

What is the last 2% of your product or service that customers actually remember, recommend, or pay a premium for? That is your real USP. Everything else is table stakes.

Law 3: Theory of Prioritisation (Exponential, Not Linear)

The book uses a weight analogy: a scale shows 10g → 100g → 1000g. Most people manage their priorities linearly (everything gets roughly equal time). Great designers think exponentially: a 1000g item deserves 100× more attention than a 10g item. This law is what makes the Convergence step of the process work — you must ruthlessly delete 80% of ideas.

Memory Aid — The 3 Laws Sequence

FIRST simplify (Less is More) → THEN find your differentiator (Last 2%) → THEN prioritise exponentially (1000g). This is the sequence: declutter, differentiate, concentrate.

Design Mind: SEPIA · DCAFE · VAL
13 forces that determine whether design thinking succeeds or fails

4.0 Anatomy of Knowledge

Core Definitions

Knowledge Packets (KPs): Unit measures of knowledge — the smallest discrete piece of knowledge you hold. A fact, a skill step, a memory.

Knowledge Clusters (KCs): Groups of KPs with similar characteristics or belonging to the same competency domain. Examples: Marketing KC, Finance KC, Legal KC, Technology KC.

Comfort Zone: The boundary that contains your familiar KCs. Operating within it requires minimal effort but produces barely acceptable results (Oxford Dictionary definition: "a settled method of working that yields only barely acceptable results").

4.1 SEPIA — The 5 Forces of Growth That DRIVE Design

S E P I A
S Skill
Set pattern of KPs through training
E Expertise
Depth in KC; subconscious mastery
P Perspective
Aerial view above the KC plane
I Idea
New connections of KPs across clusters
A Alignment
Multiple KCs converge toward one vision

Detailed Explanations of Each SEPIA Force

  • Skill: A set pattern of Knowledge Packets in a defined Knowledge Cluster, connected in a structured manner through training. Example: A car mechanic has a skill set — a precise sequence of KPs for diagnosing and fixing an engine. Skill is the foundation.
  • Expertise: When a skill is used repeatedly, expertise builds. It is depth of knowledge within a cluster. An eye surgeon who once took 45 minutes for cataract surgery now completes it in under 15 minutes with greater accuracy — that is expertise removing redundant steps. Key insight: Expertise gives the ability to observe patterns.
  • Perspective: The ability to step above the KC plane and gain an aerial view of multiple clusters and their connections. Unlike expertise (depth), perspective is height. It requires a substantially detached mind — looking at a problem from totally outside it. A CEO sees what individual department heads cannot; a consultant sees what the insider cannot.
  • Idea: Connecting KPs in unexpected new arrangements. Two types:
    • Intra-space: New connections within the same KC
    • Inter-space: Connections across different KCs (the most powerful)
    Examples: Using a car engine for an elevator. Using internet protocol for e-commerce. Expertise and Perspective are the base from which ideas emerge.
  • Alignment: When multiple Knowledge Clusters converge above the plane to achieve a common objective or vision. This is what makes large organisations design successfully — different functions aligned to one north star.

4.2 DCAFE — The 5 Frictional Forces That SLOW Design

As operations scale, friction increases. Friction is not purely negative — the most important system in a high-speed car is its braking system. Friction can be retarding or creative depending on how it is channelled.

D C A F E
D Doubt
Antidote: Use as tool to spot failure points
C Conflict
Antidote: Dialogue converts to creative conflict
A Anger
Antidote: Acceptance
F Fear
Antidote: Courage and conviction
E Ego
Antidote: Humility and unlearning

Detailed Explanations of Each DCAFE Force

D — Doubt

Defined as uncertainty or indecision in opinion or belief. Expressions: "Have I taken the right decision? Do I have the right tools?" Doubt becomes a creative force when flipped — used as a diagnostic tool to spot failure points before they occur. The antidote to paralyzing doubt is trusting your own judgement while using doubt constructively.

C — Conflict (with Story)

Story: A mother makes a beautiful yellow dress for her daughter. The daughter wants to wear her pink dress to a party. Just 30 minutes before leaving, conflict erupts. Both argue. The joyful mood is destroyed; they skip the party entirely.

Lesson: Conflict is simply a difference of opinion, perspective, or expectation. Most conflicts are suppressed rather than resolved. Dialogue — not debate — is the tool that converts frictional conflict into creative conflict. When the mother and daughter had genuinely listened to each other's desires, they could have designed a third option.

A — Anger

Anger is a symbol of helplessness, not power. The quote: "He who angers you, conquers you" (Elizabeth Kenny). The visual of an Anger Meter shows incidents accumulating until a blowup threshold is crossed. The antidote is Acceptance — accepting the complexity and ambiguity inherent in the design journey.

F — Fear

Fear of going wrong · fear of failure · fear of commitment · fear of the unknown. Fear drives leaders toward consensus decisions — the safest, least offensive option, which is almost never the best design. The antidote is Courage and Conviction in understanding and delivering the stated and unstated needs of the customer.

E — Ego (The Zen Tea Cup Story)

Story: A wealthy, important man visits a Zen master seeking wisdom. The master serves tea and keeps pouring even as the cup overflows. The visitor shouts, "The cup is full!" The master smiles: "You are like this tea cup, so full that nothing more can be added. Come back to me with an empty mind."

Ego is the "I know it all" roadblock to learning outside the Comfort Zone. It prevents new KPs from entering. The antidote is Humility and Unlearning — making your knowledge container porous. Unlearning is as important as learning; Alvin Toffler noted this, as did Greek philosopher Antisthenes (pupil of Socrates) in 445–365 BC — the principle has not changed in 2,400 years.

4.3 VAL — The 3 Capacity Levers

V A L
V — Vulnerability

Comfort Zone defined: "A situation where one feels safe or at ease; a settled method of working that requires little effort and yields only barely acceptable results." People typically reach this zone after 7–10 years in an organisation.

The power: When exposed to vulnerability — risk of being attacked or harmed physically or emotionally — the human mind thinks 10 times faster than usual (10X Capacity). Evidence from everyday life: Your thinking is sharper in a new city than your home city. Your execution accelerates dramatically just before a deadline. Arun Jain's personal example: moving from Polaris to Intellect was a deliberate choice of vulnerability that led to personal growth.

A — Appreciation

"Whatever you appreciate, appreciates."

A survey of 200 extraordinary performers out of 12,000 employees at Polaris revealed 3 elements that shifted them from ordinary to extraordinary: Trust/Appreciation from someone · Vision they believed in · Space provided by their supervisor.

The Self Worth Meter diagram shows: appreciation raises the "temperature" (Brilliant! → Fantastic! → Well Done!), while criticism drops it (Not Good! → Drop It!). The phrase "Main Hoon Na...!" ("I've got you / you're supported") captures the spirit of appreciative leadership.

L — Limiting Beliefs (The Elephant Story)

Story: A man passes a group of elephants held only by a small rope tied to their front leg. No chains, no cages. The elephant could easily break free. A trainer explains: "When they were very young and much smaller, we used the same size rope. At that age, it held them. As they grew, they were conditioned to believe they cannot break away — so they never try."

Lesson: Past failures create Holding KPs — Knowledge Packets that restrict potential. These "holding KPs" accumulate through external reinforcement ("you can't do this") and self-reinforcement ("I failed before, so I'll fail again"). They form Limiting Beliefs that function as capacity holders, keeping us tied to a rope that we could easily snap.

Step 1: Empathise (Feel)

The starting point is always understanding desirability — from the customer's perspective, not the designer's. Key insight: "We start solving symptoms rather than the actual problem." The more time spent crystallising desirability, the better the problem-solving.

Two Modes: Cartographer + Detective

As a Cartographer: Plot all elements and actors of the system on a sheet. Create the Level 0 (L0) Macro Framework — the bird's eye view of all stakeholders and their relationships. Ask What, Who, and Where.

As a Detective: Wear the detective's hat to find the real issue or desirability buried under the symptoms. Look for unstated needs. Consider Design Mind elements: Skills, Expertise, Perspectives, Doubts, Conflicts, Fears, Comfort Zones, Limiting Beliefs, Biases.

Tools: Persona Mapping · Empathy Mapping · L0 Architecture · Existing research data sets · Competitor data

Step 2: Define (Synthesise)

The Define step applies Law 1 (Less is More) and Law 3 (Prioritisation) to converge from all empathy data onto the real problem statement.

  • 5 Whys Tool: Ask Why for every answer. Keep asking "Why?" until you reach boredom or irritation — that's when you've hit the root cause. Ask questions starting with "Why?"
  • Define in 5–7 words: Brevity creates focus. Forcing the problem into 5–7 words forces prioritisation.
  • Paraphrase Positively: "Reduce Attrition" → "Enhance Retention." Positive framing opens the mind to possibilities instead of closing it around problems.
  • Role: The Reporter — asking the right questions, not assuming answers.

Step 3: Diverge (Ideate)

Divergence means bringing multiple expertises and multiple perspectives simultaneously to generate ideas. No judgement allowed — "No idea is bad" is the governing principle.

  • Round 1 — Positive Patterns (Green Post-its): Collect best practices from within your organisation, competitor organisations, and other industries. Cross-industry ideas bring the strongest new perspectives.
  • Round 2 — Anti-Patterns (Red/Pink Post-its): What fails consistently in real-life situations? Anti-patterns are repeated failures that nobody is solving. These are often the richest source of breakthrough ideas.
  • Group into sub-groups: Divide participants into multiple groups to maximise diversity. Challenge all existing assumptions, biases, processes, and policies.
  • Tools: Brainstorming · Pattern Cards · Anti-Pattern Cards

Step 4: Converge (Connect the Dots)

At this stage you have 50–200 ideas. Time to apply the 3rd Law of Design Thinking ruthlessly.

  • A — Create Affinity Clusters: Paste post-its on visible boards; let the group make grouping choices. Ideas become owned by the group, not by individuals (eliminates the "my idea" syndrome).
  • B — Connect Similar Ideas: When multiple ideas are connected, they move from idea to solution. The group starts owning solutions.
  • C — Classify Ideas: Quick Impact · Bold Impact · Long-Lasting Impact
  • D — Delete 80%: Use the 10g/100g/1000g prioritisation framework. Multi-voting by the group. Ruthlessly eliminate everything that isn't 1000g.
  • E — Prototype: Build and test the 2–3 surviving solutions. May require returning to Step 2 or 3 if results don't produce a "wow" feeling.

Step 5: Communicate (Tell Your Story)

A solution that cannot be communicated cannot be institutionalised. The movie director analogy is instructive — even the greatest film needs masterful marketing and distribution.

  • A — Naming: Choose keywords that define the solution. New vocabulary gets attention. Example: "Shift Left" instead of "proactive planning" — a terminology shift that transformed delivery excellence at Intellect Design Arena.
  • B — Packaging: Wrap the solution in a coherent form.
  • C — Story Boarding: Create a storyboard like a movie director. This unlocks hidden creative talent.
  • D — Presentation: Present to stakeholders with the story.
  • E — Distribution: White papers · Advertising · Social media · Group presentations · Webinars

06
Design Frameworks
The "knowledge shelving and wiring" system that makes solutions repeatable

6.1 Definition of a Framework

Definition

A Framework is "knowledge shelving and wiring" — a taxonomy that places knowledge into appropriate shelves, then connects (wires) those shelves to create actionable, navigable architecture.

6.2 The Four Knowledge Architecture Levels

L0
Macro Reference Architecture: The identifier for each major column/shelf. The highest-level view — what are the major domains? (e.g., Brand, Customer, Leadership, Product, Execution, Finance)
L1
Column Details: Each L0 column is expanded one level deeper. What are the components within each major domain?
L2
User Journeys: L1 components are expanded into the actual journeys that users experience. How does a customer move through this domain step by step?
L3
Micro Knowledge — Patterns & Anti-Patterns: The granular level. What works reliably (patterns)? What fails reliably (anti-patterns)? This is where expertise and last-2% live.

6.4 Types of Design Frameworks

  • Business Framework: Maps the entire business architecture (e.g., the Global Universal Banking framework showing all banking functions from Branch to ATM to Core Banking)
  • Knowledge Framework: The BELIEF framework — a 6-column L0 wired architecture for assessing organisational health
  • Execution Framework: D-3 OTIF — balancing On Time (Planning, Monitoring) and In Full (User Journey, Engineering Blueprint), filtered through Design, Diagnostics, Patterns, Anti-Patterns to achieve Higher Throughput
  • Performance Management Framework: For measuring and managing ongoing execution
  • Community Development Framework: Mission Samriddhi — applying design thinking to social development with 50+ NGOs

07
Space, Methods and Tools
The physical and behavioural environment that sustains design thinking

7.1 The Physical Design Space

The environment is not neutral — it actively shapes the quality of thinking. Design Thinking requires a deliberately curated physical space:

  • Visual Devices: For stating and displaying problems
  • Tactile Devices: Cuboids, drawing on large sheets (physical manipulation of ideas)
  • 9-Screen Video Walls: For displaying multiple perspectives simultaneously
  • Large Whiteboards: Benzene structure arrangements for free-form thinking
  • Interactive Screens: For digital collaboration
  • Magnetic Boards: For flexible idea organisation
  • Role Play Setups: For empathy exercises and perspective shifts

7.2 Growth Mindset Methods

Seven methods for continuously expanding the capacity to design:

Active Listening & Reading
Absorb without filtering through past knowledge
Dialogue & Asking Questions
Questions starting with Why, What, Who, Where
Observing Patterns & Anti-Patterns
Systematic noticing of what works and fails
Diagnostics — 5 Whys
Root cause drill-down until irritated
Decisions — 10g/100g/1000g
Exponential prioritisation in all decisions
Role Modelling
Learning by observing masters in action

Plus: Having a Sounding Board — a trusted person outside your KC who can provide perspective.

7.3 Sustainable Thinking Shift Tool Set (8 Tools)

Tools to institutionalise the shift in thinking beyond workshops:

T1 — Circle Time

Regular group reflection sessions to maintain the design thinking culture.

T2 — L0–L3 Architecture

Continuously maintaining and updating the knowledge framework levels.

T3 — Persona End State Visualisation

Vividly imagining the persona's desired end state to maintain empathy focus.

T4 — End State Visualisation

Full visualisation of the desired outcome to guide design direction.

T5 — Journey Map

Mapping the full user journey to identify design intervention points.

T6 — Anti-Pattern / Pattern Cards

Structured cards capturing what consistently fails vs. what consistently works.

T7 — Shift Left / Shift Down / Shift Out

Proactively move problems to earlier stages in the process before they become expensive. "Shift Left" = catch issues in design, not deployment.

T8 — Theory of Constraints

Identify the single biggest bottleneck in the system and focus all improvement energy there.

The Five-Layer System

The genius of UnMukt is that it is not a linear methodology but a living system. Here is how the five components interact:

Layer 1: The Seed — The Design Mind

Everything begins with the human mind. Before any process is useful, the mind must be prepared. SEPIA forces must be cultivated (Skill → Expertise → Perspective → Idea → Alignment), while DCAFE forces must be diagnosed and neutralised (Doubt, Conflict, Anger, Fear, Ego). The VAL levers expand capacity. Without this inner work, the 5-step process is just another workshop.

Layer 2: The Engine — The 5-Step Process

Once the mind is prepared through unlearning and humility (empty cup), it enters the Design Process: Empathise → Define → Diverge → Converge → Communicate. This is the operational machinery. Note: Step 1 (Empathise) uses Design Mind tools (persona, empathy map). Steps 3 and 4 use SEPIA forces (diverse expertise) and manage DCAFE risks (no judgement in Diverge).

Layer 3: The Governor — The Three Laws

As ideas flow through the engine, the Three Laws act as quality governors. Law 1 (Less is More) governs Convergence — ruthlessly eliminating 80%. Law 2 (Last 2% = 200%) governs the Communicate step — finding the USP. Law 3 (Prioritisation) governs both Define and Convergence — 1000g items only.

Layer 4: The Output — Frameworks

Solutions from the process are not left floating. They are wired into structured Frameworks (L0→L1→L2→L3, BELIEF, D-3 OTIF) that make the solution repeatable, scalable, and teachable. This is how a design insight becomes institutional knowledge.

Layer 5: The Soil — Space & Growth Mindset

All of this occurs within a deliberately curated physical Design Space using visual, tactile, and interactive tools. The 7 Growth Mindset practices and 8 Sustainable Thinking Shift tools maintain the ecosystem — ensuring the system keeps running, not just producing a one-time workshop output.

The Dialogue-Conflict-Design Triangle

One of the most elegant insights in the book: Conflict (DCAFE) seems purely negative but is actually the raw material for design when processed through Dialogue (Chapter 1). The mother-daughter dress story shows frictional conflict. Had they engaged in Dialogue, they would have created a third option — a designed solution. This connects Chapter 1's Listening → Chapter 4's DCAFE → Chapter 5's Diverge step (where conflicting ideas are celebrated, not suppressed).

The Vulnerability-Expertise-Idea Chain

Choosing Vulnerability (VAL Lever 1) expands comfort zone → which exposes you to new KCs → which builds new Expertise (SEPIA) → which creates the base from which new Ideas (SEPIA) emerge. The author's personal journey from Nucleus to Polaris to Intellect is a lived demonstration of this chain.

The Anti-Pattern Bridge

Anti-patterns appear in three separate parts of the book: in Definition 3 (observing what fails), in the Diverge step (red post-it cards), and in the L3 level of frameworks. This repetition is intentional — what fails is data; what consistently fails across organisations is a design opportunity nobody has seized yet.
